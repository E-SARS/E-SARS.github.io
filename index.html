Description and Motivation

This workshop focuses on advancing safe and quality-assured embodied robotic systems. Embodied systems‚Äîincluding autonomous robots, self-driving vehicles, robotic arms, and humanoid robots‚Äîare increasingly deployed in safety-critical real-world scenarios. Ensuring their trustworthiness‚Äîencompassing safety, reliability, and predictable behavior‚Äîremains a pressing challenge. Despite notable progress in perception, reasoning, and control, many AI-based robotic systems still operate as ‚Äúblack boxes,‚Äù often exhibiting unpredictable behaviors. Failures can emerge from complex sensorimotor interactions, adversarial inputs, or novel environments, leading to safety incidents and diminished user trust. Recent studies have shown that the intricate interplay among sensors, actuators, and AI components exposes embodied agents to a wide range of vulnerabilities, including sensor spoofing, adversarial attacks, and planning failures, which may result in unauthorized actions or physical harm. These risks underscore the urgent need for robust safety measures throughout system deployment. Rigorous testing and validation are essential to identify and mitigate such failure modes before real-world use. In high-stakes domains like autonomous driving, testing across diverse and complex scenarios is not only critical for verifying safety but also for earning public trust. Without confidence in their safety and reliability, the widespread adoption of embodied robotic systems will remain limited and potentially hazardous.

Topics of interest

This workshop aims to unite researchers and industry experts in developing systematic methodologies for testing, validating, and certifying the trustworthiness of intelligent robots. Topics will cover a broad range of topic interests in perception, control, testing, assurance and safety for embodied robotic systems, including but not limited to:
  • Quality assurance for robotics that rely on artificial intelligence
  • LLM for safety-critical scenario generation for autonomous driving (AGI, World model, Code generation)
  • Vision‚Äìlanguage‚Äìaction integration
  • Safety-critical reinforcement learning
  • Verification and assurance of autonomous robots
  • Formal verification and control guarantees
  • Evaluation benchmarks for real-world safety
  • Human in the loop trust evaluation, integrating user studies and feedback to measure and improve perceived reliability
  • Ethical and societal implications, examining how testing and assurance connect with ethics, regulation and public acceptance
  • Robust Robotic perception in dynamic/uncertain environments

Bio: Sinan Hasirlioglu received his Ph.D. in Engineering Science from Johannes Kepler University Linz, Linz, Austria, in 2020. His doctoral research focused on simulation-based testing and validation of automotive surround sensors under adverse weather conditions. In the same year, Sinan joined Robert Bosch GmbH, where he contributed to the Urban Automated Driving project with a focus on System Validation and Release. Testing of Autonomous Driving Systems: From Simulated to Real-world Environments
Abstract: Autonomous driving systems (ADS) require extensive testing to ensure safety, reliability, and robustness across a wide spectrum of operational conditions. This talk introduces the state of the art in testing and evaluation of ADS, with a particular focus on simulation-based techniques and the challenges of transferring results from simulation to real-world environments. We discuss approaches for scenario generation and performance assessment, as well as current limitations in realism, sensor modeling, and non-deterministic behavior. Finally, the talk explores emerging research directions, such as sim2real consistency analysis, uncertainty quantification, and AI-driven test generation.

The Advantages of Using Scenarios as Part of the Specification in Automated Driving
Abstract: For the development and deployment of Driving Automation Systems, manufacturers use scenarios to decompose the complex world into smaller pieces. Traditionally, scenarios focus on verification and validation processes. This talk introduces the Scenario as Specification approach, which connects requirements to scenario-based descriptions of target behavior. By combining target behavior with environmental context, scenarios become a foundation for safety engineering and verification and validation. Practical examples illustrate the key concepts and the motivation behind this approach.

Towards Robust and Efficient Autonomous Driving Systems from the Lens of Software Engineering
Abstract: Autonomous driving systems operate in complex, unpredictable environments where accuracy alone is insufficient. Safe deployment requires robustness, efficiency, fault tolerance, and systematic testing. This talk discusses how software engineering perspectives help bridge the gap between ML model performance and real-world dependable autonomy. Dr. Yang introduces recent work on robustness frameworks for ADS perception, stress-testing perceptual modules, and detecting high-latency behaviors. These findings highlight the importance of engineering-driven analysis in building safer and more reliable autonomous systems.
