Description and Motivation

This workshop focuses on advancing safe and quality-assured embodied robotic systems. Embodied systems‚Äîincluding autonomous robots, self-driving vehicles, robotic arms, and humanoid robots‚Äîare increasingly deployed in safety-critical real-world scenarios. Ensuring their trustworthiness‚Äîencompassing safety, reliability, and predictable behavior‚Äîremains a pressing challenge. Despite notable progress in perception, reasoning, and control, many AI-based robotic systems still operate as ‚Äúblack boxes,‚Äù often exhibiting unpredictable behaviors. Failures can emerge from complex sensorimotor interactions, adversarial inputs, or novel environments, leading to safety incidents and diminished user trust. Recent studies have shown that the intricate interplay among sensors, actuators, and AI components exposes embodied agents to a wide range of vulnerabilities, including sensor spoofing, adversarial attacks, and planning failures, which may result in unauthorized actions or physical harm. These risks underscore the urgent need for robust safety measures throughout system deployment. Rigorous testing and validation are essential to identify and mitigate such failure modes before real-world use. In high-stakes domains like autonomous driving, testing across diverse and complex scenarios is not only critical for verifying safety but also for earning public trust. Without confidence in their safety and reliability, the widespread adoption of embodied robotic systems will remain limited and potentially hazardous.

Topics of interest

This workshop aims to unite researchers and industry experts in developing systematic methodologies for testing, validating, and certifying the trustworthiness of intelligent robots. Topics will cover a broad range of topic interests in testing, assurance and safety for embodied robotic systems, including but not limited to:
  • Automated generation of test scenarios
  • Quality assurance for robotics that rely on artificial intelligence, with best practices and pipelines that preserve reliability in systems that learn
  • Formal verification and control guarantees, where formal methods or control theoretic techniques establish provable safety or stability
  • Evaluation metrics and benchmarks, emphasising quantitative indicators and standardised tasks for trustworthiness
  • Human in the loop trust evaluation, integrating user studies and feedback to measure and improve perceived reliability; and, time permitting
  • Ethical and societal implications, examining how testing and assurance connect with ethics, regulation and public acceptance
